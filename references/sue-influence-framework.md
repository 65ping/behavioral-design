# SUE Influence Framework: Four Forces

Maps the psychological forces that keep users in their current state or pull them toward a new behavior. Use this before designing interventions — it diagnoses *why* users aren't changing, not just *where* they're stopping.

---

## The Four Forces

### Pains (Push away from current state)

What is frustrating, inconvenient, or costly about what the user currently does?

**Design responses:**
- **Resolve** — Remove the pain in your product
- **Highlight** — Make the user more aware of the pain they're already experiencing

**Questions to ask:**
- What is the user frustrated by in their current approach?
- What does their current behavior cost them in time, money, or stress?
- Are they aware of these costs, or have they normalized them?

**Example:** Users manually copy data between tools weekly. Pain: time wasted, errors made. Most users have stopped noticing — the pain is real but subconscious. Design response: surface the cost ("You've manually re-entered this 47 times this year").

---

### Gains (Pull toward new behavior)

What is appealing, beneficial, or desirable about the new behavior?

**Design response:**
- **Enhance** — Make the gain more salient, more immediate, and more personally relevant

**Questions to ask:**
- What's the real benefit of the new behavior?
- Is the benefit immediate or delayed?
- Is the benefit specific enough to be motivating?
- Does it connect to something the user already cares about?

**Example:** Automation saves 3 hours/week. That gain is real but abstract. Enhance by calculating: "3 hours = one extra afternoon with your team per month." Concrete, personal, near-term.

---

### Anxieties (Fears holding back)

What is the user worried about if they make the change?

**Design response:**
- **Resolve** — Address anxieties directly in the design

**Common anxiety types:**
- "What if I lose my data / settings / work in progress?"
- "What if this is harder than what I do now?"
- "What if I make the wrong choice and can't undo it?"
- "What if this doesn't work for my specific situation?"
- "What will my team think if something goes wrong?"

**Questions to ask:**
- What risks does the user perceive (even if they're overestimated)?
- What objections do support teams hear before users convert?
- What questions appear in user research at this decision point?

**Example:** Users worry that switching to automated workflows will break existing processes. Design response: add "preview before applying," show reversibility, include "10,000 teams have made this switch successfully."

---

### Comforts (Pull toward current behavior)

What is habitual, familiar, or emotionally comfortable about the current behavior?

**Design responses:**
- **Replace** — Offer equivalent comfort in the new behavior
- **Piggyback** — Connect the new behavior to the existing comfortable routine

**Questions to ask:**
- What does the user get from their current behavior beyond the functional outcome?
- Is the current approach a deeply embedded habit?
- Is there social or identity comfort in the current approach (e.g., "I'm a spreadsheet person")?

**Example:** Users are comfortable with manual spreadsheets because they feel in control and can see everything. Design response: the new tool must provide the same sense of control and visibility — don't just automate, make the automation transparent.

## Using the Four Forces in Design

### Mapping exercise

For any behavior change you're designing for, fill in the four forces from the user's perspective:

| Force | User's experience | Design response |
|-------|------------------|----------------|
| Pain | What's wrong with their current situation? | Resolve or highlight |
| Gain | What do they stand to benefit? | Enhance specificity and immediacy |
| Anxiety | What are they afraid of? | Address directly |
| Comfort | What do they like about their current approach? | Replace or piggyback |

### Force prioritization

Not all forces are equal for every user segment. Identify the one or two dominant forces and design interventions for those first.

### Common patterns

**Anxiety-dominant situation** — User is motivated and capable but scared.
→ Focus on reassurance, reversibility, social proof of safety

**Comfort-dominant situation** — User has no pain and is satisfied with current behavior.
→ Highlight unseen costs, or offer the new behavior as a supplement rather than a replacement

**Gain-deficit situation** — User doesn't see the upside clearly.
→ Make benefits more immediate, specific, and personally relevant

**Pain-insufficient situation** — User is aware of the problem but not motivated enough to act.
→ Increase salience of the pain or connect it to something they care deeply about

---

## Four Forces vs. SPARK × WANT × AGAIN × CAN

These frameworks are complementary, not competing.

- **Four Forces** → *Why* users aren't changing (motivational landscape diagnosis)
- **SPARK × WANT × AGAIN × CAN** → *What* to design (intervention architecture)

Diagnose with Four Forces. Design with SPARK × WANT × AGAIN × CAN.
