# Ethics Checklist for Behavioral Interventions

Use before launching any behavioral design intervention. Document responses for team alignment.

---

## Core Distinction

**Nudge** (acceptable): A design change that makes a beneficial behavior easier or more salient, while fully preserving the user's freedom to choose otherwise. The user's long-term interest is served.

**Manipulation** (not acceptable): A design change that exploits cognitive biases to drive behavior against the user's interests, or that conceals its intent from the user.

**The test**: Would the user endorse this intervention if they understood exactly what it was doing and why?

---

## Five-Point Checklist

### 1. User Benefit Test
- [ ] Does this intervention primarily benefit the user?
- [ ] Does it serve the user's long-term wellbeing, not just their immediate impulse or the business's short-term metric?
- [ ] Is the business benefit aligned with user benefit, or in tension with it?

If in tension: Requires explicit justification and senior design/product review. Not automatically disqualifying, but must be documented and weighed carefully.

---

### 2. Transparency Test
- [ ] Would users object if they could see exactly what this design is doing?
- [ ] Is the intent hidden or disguised?
- [ ] If a journalist described this design choice, would it be reported as a dark pattern?

Red flags: Hidden pre-selection of paid options. Artificial urgency ("Only 2 left!" when false). Confirmshaming. Making opting out deliberately difficult.

---

### 3. Autonomy Test
- [ ] Can users still make a different choice freely?
- [ ] Is the alternative choice hidden, obscured, or penalized?
- [ ] Does this restrict genuine choice, or simply make one option easier?

---

### 4. Vulnerability Test
- [ ] Could this intervention harm users in a vulnerable state (financial stress, health anxiety, emotional distress)?
- [ ] Is this targeting a population that may be less able to recognize the nudge?

---

### 5. Reversibility Test
- [ ] Can users undo the behavior if they change their mind?
- [ ] Are consequences of the action clearly communicated before the user commits?

---

## Escalation Guide

| Risk level | Criteria | Action |
|-----------|----------|--------|
| Low | Clearly beneficial to user, fully transparent, choice preserved | Proceed |
| Medium | Business/user interest tension, or nudge feels aggressive | Document rationale, review with design lead |
| High | Potential to harm users, opacity about intent, targets vulnerable users | Full ethics review; likely redesign required |

---

## Dark Patterns to Avoid

- **Confirmshaming** - Making the decline option emotionally punishing ("No thanks, I don't want to save money")
- **Roach motel** - Easy to enter, very hard to leave
- **Hidden costs** - Revealing charges late in the flow after the user has committed psychologically
- **Misdirection** - Drawing attention away from information the user needs
- **Artificial scarcity/urgency** - Creating time or supply pressure that isn't genuine
- **Disguised ads or pre-selections** - Making promoted options look organic, or pre-checking paid upgrades
- **Privacy zuckering** - Getting users to share more data than they'd want if they understood the implications
- **Trick questions** - Confusing double-negatives or pre-checked opt-outs that are easy to miss
